The Effects of Smoking Tobacco on the World(test)

Tobacco kills 8 million people every year according to the World Health Organisation, with many harmful effects including cancer, disease, decay, heart attacks and strokes. This report seeks to show how smoking impacts various countries around the world. This includes the deaths caused by smoking and the affordability of each cigarettes worldwide. It also compares the Australian statistics with data from the top 10 most powerful countries in the world.

Smoking Death Rate (2019) VS Affordability of cigarettes (2020)

The red choropleth on the left illustrates the number of deaths caused by smoking for every 100,000 people living in the country. For example Greenland has the highest death rate of 193.32 deaths caused by smoking per 100,000 people. The turquoise choropleth map displays the affordability of cigarettes in each country in which affordability is defined as the percentage of GDP required to buy 2000 of the most common brand on cigarettes in the country. Total number of deaths caused by the top 10 most fatal risk factors

Smoking is the second largest killer in the world with 1.25 billion deaths from 1990 to 2019 with only high systolic blood pressure being ahead. However these deaths are a self induced direct consequence of smoking which can be prevented. In 2019, 7.69 million people died from smoking while 1.3 million deaths was caused by second hand smoke, which shows how smokers can endanger people around them.

Change in smoking death rates and cigarette affordability over time in powerful countries

Australia is less affected by the harm caused by smoking as they have addressed the danger more effectively than the top 10 most powerful countries in the world. The line graph on the left shows the change each year in deaths per 100,000 people caused by smoking from 1990 to 2019. Australia has had the lowest smoking death rate since 1999 out of those countries. This can be partially explained by the effort to reduce accessibility to cigarettes by making them less afforable through an increase in taxes on tobacco. This is shown in the ranged dot plot on the right which visualises the change in percentage of GDP per capita required to purchase 2000 of the most common cigarettes from 2010 to 2020. Australia saw the largest increase in cost(1.94% increase) and holds the least afforable price on cigarettes (3.89%). Despite having the highest death rate caused by smoking, China is the only powerful country to have had a significanat price decrease in the cost of cigarettes
